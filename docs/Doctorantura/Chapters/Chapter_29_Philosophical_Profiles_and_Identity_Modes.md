## 29.2.1 HOW Identity Modes Are Implemented (Mechanics Layer)

Philosophical Profiles & Identity Modes™ are not symbolic labels or stylistic presets.
They are **mechanically enforced behavioural constraint sets** that operate as a mid-layer
between reasoning output and action eligibility.

Each Identity Mode is implemented as a bounded configuration consisting of:

1) **Priority Weights**
   - ethical weighting (risk vs benefit),
   - explanatory depth requirement,
   - refusal threshold sensitivity.

2) **Permitted Reasoning Frames**
   - causal reasoning,
   - normative evaluation,
   - precautionary logic,
   - or evidentiary scepticism.

3) **Mandatory Output Constraints**
   - tone discipline,
   - claim strength limits,
   - escalation language requirements,
   - disclaimer enforcement when required.

The Identity Mode therefore shapes **how conclusions are formed, framed, and released** —
not merely how they are phrased.

---

## 29.2.2 HOW Identity Mode Selection Occurs (Selection Engine)

Identity Mode selection is a governed process, not an autonomous preference.

The selection pipeline operates as follows:

1) **Context Classification**
   - domain (engineering, governance, safety, information integrity),
   - risk level,
   - public vs internal use,
   - presence of protected human domains.

2) **Mandate Cross-Check**
   - Human Mandate Layer™ permissions,
   - enterprise vs public access,
   - jurisdictional or contractual limits.

3) **Receipt Obligation Check**
   - whether DRS™ or OPS™ thresholds are triggered,
   - whether explanation depth must be maximised or constrained.

4) **Default-Safe Resolution**
   - when multiple profiles apply,
   - the system must select the **most restrictive coherent profile**,
   - or escalate to human authority.

At no point may the system select an Identity Mode
that reduces safety, accountability, or receipt obligations.

---

## 29.2.3 HOW Identity Modes Are Enforced at Runtime

Once selected, an Identity Mode is enforced through:

- **Reasoning Filters**
  Preventing disallowed argument structures or rhetorical shortcuts.

- **Action Gates**
  Blocking outputs that exceed the profile’s authority scope.

- **Escalation Hooks**
  Forcing hand-off to human authority or higher governance layers
  when confidence, mandate, or coherence is insufficient.

If an output violates the active Identity Mode,
it is rejected **before** it can propagate.

This makes Identity Modes enforceable law,
not advisory guidance.

## 29.2.4 HOW Identity Modes Interact With Higher Law

Identity Modes are always subordinate to:

- Law of Ethical Irreversibility™ (LEI = 1™),
- Receipt-First Doctrine,
- Third Guardian Firewall™ intervention logic,
- Hardware and execution-level governance.

If an Identity Mode would allow an action
that violates a higher law,
the action is refused and logged.

Identity Modes may **never override**:
- coherence constraints,
- safety vetoes,
- or mandate limits.

## 29.2.5 HOW Conflicts Between Profiles Are Resolved

When two or more Identity Modes appear applicable:

1) **Risk Supremacy Rule**
   - the profile with the strictest harm-minimisation wins.

2) **Authority Supremacy Rule**
   - explicit human instruction overrides automatic selection.

3) **Opacity Fallback Rule**
   - if interpretability drops, the system must simplify,
     restrict claims, or refuse to act.

All conflict resolutions are eligible for
receipt emission when they affect public legitimacy.

---

## 29.2.6 HOW Identity Drift Is Prevented Over Time

To prevent gradual behavioural drift:

- Identity Mode selection is re-evaluated per session and per task.
- Historical identity decisions may be sampled by ASA™ for drift detection.
- Any statistically significant deviation in tone, risk posture,
  or refusal behaviour triggers review.

This ensures the system cannot “slide” into a different character
without detection.

## 29.2.7 WHY Identity Modes Are Court-Legible

Identity Modes are legible because:

- selection criteria are explicit,
- enforcement rules are deterministic,
- refusals and escalations are reconstructable,
- and identity conflicts leave audit traces.

An external evaluator can therefore reconstruct:

- which identity was active,
- why it was selected,
- what it permitted or forbade,
- and whether it acted lawfully.

This satisfies institutional and court-grade scrutiny.
